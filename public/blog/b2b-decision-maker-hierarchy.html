B2B Decision Maker Hierarchy: Who Signs Off in 14 Industries

February 10, 2026·10 min read·~1022 words

Sending cold email to the wrong person is not just inefficient — it can permanently close the door at a company. Understanding the buying hierarchy in each industry before you target determines whether your outreach reaches someone with authority to act or someone who cannot move forward without six layers of approval.

How to Read a B2B Buying Committee

Every B2B purchase involves at least three distinct roles that may be held by the same person at a small company or by different individuals at an enterprise. The economic buyer holds budget authority and makes the final financial decision. The user buyer will use the product daily and has strong influence over the evaluation. The champion is the internal advocate who wants the solution and drives the evaluation process internally. The blocker is any stakeholder who can veto the deal — legal, IT, procurement, or a competing budget owner.

Cold outreach that reaches only one of these roles rarely converts to a deal without the others engaging. The most effective cold email strategies identify the champion-level contact first, reach them, and then expand outreach to the economic buyer once internal interest is established.

Technology and SaaS: CTO, VP Engineering, Head of Product

For B2B software tools targeting technology companies: user buyers are engineering managers and developers; champions are typically VP Engineering, Head of Platform, or Director of DevOps; economic buyers are CTOs at startups and VPs or C-suite at scale-ups. Security tools are reviewed by a separate track: CISO at enterprise, Head of Security at mid-market. Procurement involvement begins at $25,000 annual contract value and becomes dominant above $100,000.

For SaaS tools targeting sales and marketing functions at technology companies: the champion is the RevOps Director or Head of Growth; economic buyer is CRO or VP Sales; user buyers are SDRs and AEs. Sequencing: reach the RevOps Director first with operational framing, then expand to CRO with business case framing once internal interest is confirmed.

Healthcare: CMO, CFO, IT Director, Department Heads

Healthcare buying authority depends critically on deal type. Clinical technology (EHR, CDSS, remote monitoring): champions are department heads or medical directors; economic buyers are CMOs or CFOs; IT Director has veto authority on all technology. Administrative and operational software: CFO is economic buyer, COO is champion. Compliance and regulatory tools: Chief Compliance Officer has veto, CFO has budget.

Healthcare has the most complex buying committee structure of any industry. An enterprise health system sale involves a minimum of 5 to 8 stakeholders across clinical, financial, IT, and compliance functions. Cold email into healthcare should target department heads and directors as champions, not C-suite as first contact. Mid-market clinic groups have simpler hierarchies: practice administrator or managing physician is the combined champion and economic buyer.

Financial Services: CFO, CRO, Head of Operations, Compliance Officer

Investment management: for trading and portfolio management tools, the economic buyer is the CIO, champion is head of quantitative strategies or portfolio manager, compliance and risk function have veto power. Retail banking: the hierarchy is more rigid. Technology decisions follow a vendor panel process — outreach to the individual business unit head who is experiencing the problem is the most effective approach.

Digital banking improvements flow through the Chief Digital Officer or Head of Digital Innovation. Generic CTO cold email at large banks rarely converts because technology decisions are committee-driven at procurement level. Outreach calibrated to the operational decision-maker in the affected business unit consistently outperforms C-suite cold outreach in financial services.

Manufacturing: VP Operations, Plant Manager, Supply Chain Director

Manufacturing decision authority is operationally concentrated rather than C-suite concentrated. For operational efficiency solutions: Plant Manager or VP of Operations is the champion and often the economic buyer for sub-$50,000 annual spend. Above $50,000, CFO or COO sign-off is required. Supply chain solutions: VP Supply Chain or Chief Supply Chain Officer; procurement involvement at all stages. ERP and MES systems: CTO or VP IT is the technical evaluator, CFO is economic buyer, COO is champion.

Manufacturing outreach performs best when it targets the operational decision-maker directly with process-specific framing rather than C-suite with generic efficiency claims. The operational director who manages the specific problem you solve is more likely to become an internal champion than a CFO who needs to be educated on the operational context first.

Professional Services: Managing Partner, Practice Head, CFO

Law firms: technology solutions are evaluated by the Director of IT or Chief Information Officer at large firms; at mid-size firms by the Managing Partner directly. Financial decisions above $25,000 typically require partnership-level approval. Cold email to individual partners is generally low-conversion — the firm administrator or IT director is a more effective entry point.

Accounting firms are almost inaccessible during tax season (January through April). Target Q1 early (January) or post-tax season (May through June) for highest accessibility. At regional accounting firms, the managing partner and office managing partner are the combined champion and economic buyer for most technology investments.

E-commerce: Founder, VP Marketing, Head of Growth, Head of Operations

Direct-to-consumer e-commerce at under $10 million revenue: the founder or co-founder is the economic buyer and often the champion. Outreach directly to the founder with specific ROI framing and a concrete observation about their current stack achieves the highest conversion rate. Above $10 million, functional leaders emerge: Head of Growth or VP Marketing for customer acquisition and retention tools; Head of Operations or VP Supply Chain for fulfillment and logistics tools.

The decision cycle in e-commerce is short (1 to 3 weeks for sub-$10,000 annual tools) and procurement involvement is rare below $25,000 annual spend. This makes e-commerce one of the fastest-converting sectors for cold email — shorter sales cycle, direct decision-making authority, and high ROI sensitivity.

Multi-Industry Pattern: The Champion Strategy

Across all 14 industries, the highest-converting cold email outreach strategy follows a consistent pattern: identify the champion, reach them with operationally-framed copy that addresses a specific problem in their function, and then expand to the economic buyer with champion support already established. Cold email directly to the economic buyer without champion engagement produces lower conversion because economic buyers require internal champions to drive evaluation processes.

In healthcare and manufacturing, the champion is typically two to three levels below the economic buyer. In e-commerce and early-stage SaaS, the champion and economic buyer may be the same person. Calibrating your outreach to reach the champion level is the single structural change that most reliably improves cold email conversion rates across industries.
