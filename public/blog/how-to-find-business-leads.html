How to Find Business Leads — 8 Methods for B2B Prospecting

May 6, 20256 min read

Finding high-quality business leads is the front-end constraint of every B2B sales process. The method you choose determines the quality, quantity, targeting precision, and cost of your lead flow.

Method 1: Purchased B2B Contact Databases

Fastest path to a large, targeted lead list. Reputable databases provide verified emails, company names, job titles, and industry classifications for specific geographies or sectors. Evaluate on verification rate (95%+), geographic depth, industry segmentation, and recency. One-time purchases are cost-effective for defined campaigns.

Method 2: LinkedIn Sales Navigator

Most precise targeting tool available for individual high-value prospects. Filter by title, industry, company size, geography, and seniority. Limitation: 100 connection requests per week. Best combined with a purchased database — use Navigator for research and personalisation, the database for volume outreach.

Method 3: Google Maps + Local Search

Excellent for local business targeting — restaurants, retailers, clinics, service providers. Google Maps provides name, address, website, and phone for every listed business. Pair with an email finder (Hunter.io, Snov.io) to fill in email fields for businesses with a web presence.

Method 4: Industry Directories and Associations

Trade associations and certification bodies publish member contact information. These lists are self-selected (members opted in to be listed) and tend to have higher engagement rates. Examples: Chamber of Commerce directories, professional associations, SaaS marketplaces like G2 and Capterra for technology buyers.

Purchased Databases as a Starting Point

Purchasing a targeted B2B database is the fastest way to build a qualified prospect list for a new market or vertical. A verified, segmented list of 5,000 contacts in your target industry and geography can be operational within hours. Browse B2B email lists by country or USA industry databases to find options matching your target profile and ICP.

LinkedIn as a Complementary Channel

LinkedIn Sales Navigator is effective for identifying specific individuals at target accounts. It is expensive ($99-1,600/month) and requires manual effort at scale. The most effective workflow combines a purchased database for volume targeting with LinkedIn for deep research on the 100-200 highest-value accounts. Use the database for broad coverage; use LinkedIn for account-specific personalisation and depth.

Building a Systematic Prospecting Engine

A systematic B2B prospecting engine has four components: a data source (purchased database or LinkedIn), a verification step (NeverBounce or ZeroBounce), a sending infrastructure (dedicated subdomain, warmed inboxes, DKIM/SPF/DMARC configured), and a sequencing tool (Instantly, Smartlead, or Apollo). Building this infrastructure once allows you to launch new campaigns in hours for any new market or product.

Google Maps and Local Business Data

Google Maps data is an underutilised B2B lead source for locally-targeted campaigns. Every business with a Google Business Profile listing has publicly visible information: business name, category, phone number, website (which often contains contact email addresses), address, and frequently the owner name. For local service businesses — contractors, medical practices, restaurants, retailers — Google Maps is often the most comprehensive source of verified business existence.

The practical extraction workflow: search Google Maps for your target business category in your target geography, export the visible results, then enhance with website visits to extract email addresses. This approach is most effective for hyper-local campaigns (a city or metro area rather than a country) where the universe of targets is small enough to process manually or with light automation. The data provenance for Google Maps-sourced contacts is clear and publicly available, which is the cleanest compliance position.

Google Maps data has a freshness advantage over database providers for local businesses: Google's business profile update system relies on both business owners (who can update their own profile) and user-reported changes, meaning the data reflects current business status more accurately than databases that re-verify on quarterly cycles. A business that closed 6 months ago will typically have a "Permanently closed" marker on Google Maps before most database providers remove it from their records.

LinkedIn as a Research and Targeting Tool

LinkedIn Sales Navigator is the most powerful intent-based B2B prospecting tool available at its price point. The advanced search allows filtering by geography, industry, company size, seniority level, years in role, company growth rate, and recently changed jobs — the last filter being particularly valuable for identifying prospects who are newly in a role and actively evaluating vendors for their new position.

The 'recently changed jobs' filter targets people who just became decision-makers at a new employer and are in the 90-day window where they're most likely to be evaluating their current vendor relationships. A VP of Sales who just joined a new company is an ideal target for sales tools, CRM software, and B2B data providers — they're building their stack from scratch and have fresh budget authority. This targeting is not available through any other B2B data source at comparable precision.

LinkedIn data has limitations as a cold outreach source: InMail has credits limits, connection request acceptance rates vary by personalisation quality, and LinkedIn's algorithms increasingly filter automated outreach. The most effective LinkedIn workflow uses it for research and warm-up (view profile, engage with content, send connection request) while using email from a verified database for the actual cold outreach — combining LinkedIn research with email delivery.

Industry-Specific Lead Sources

Every industry has specific databases, directories, and associations that produce higher-quality leads than generic B2B databases for that vertical. Knowing the authoritative sources for your target industry dramatically reduces research time and improves data quality.

Healthcare: State medical board licensing databases are public records containing every licensed physician, dentist, nurse practitioner, and specialist in the state. These are the most authoritative sources for healthcare professional contact data — they confirm licensure status, specialty, and practice address. CMS (Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services) maintains open datasets on physicians participating in federal programmes.

Legal: State bar association membership directories contain every licensed attorney, with admission date, firm, practice areas, and contact information. Martindale-Hubbell has been rating lawyers since 1868 and maintains a comprehensive directory. Avvo combines bar directory data with user reviews and is publicly accessible.

Financial: FINRA's BrokerCheck database lists every registered investment advisor and broker-dealer representative. SEC's EDGAR contains investment advisor registration documents with contact information. State insurance commissioner databases list every licensed insurance agent.

Construction: State contractor licensing boards maintain public databases of every licensed contractor by trade and geography. These databases confirm licensure status, bond amounts, and business contact information — more reliable than self-reported data for industries where unlicensed operators are common.
